The world premiere of the new SL 129 series was one of the main attractions at the Geneva Motor Show in March 1989. Apart from the overall concept, the completely redesigned 300 SL, 300 SL 24 and 500 SL had practically nothing in common with the predecessor models of the 107 series. Just as the 350 SL had demonstrated automotive progress 18 years earlier with a wealth of innovative design details, the SL of the 129 series was also a new car in every respect. The new SL generation impressed with its harmonious and extremely successful stylistic design, which expressed dynamism and sportiness, but also comfort and elegance. On the one hand, the design was tradition-oriented and, on the other hand, pointed far into the future with creatively new detailed solutions. For example, the radiator grille, the legendary trademark of the SL models, was reinterpreted in a style that retained the traditional basic shape. While the wide chrome frame with the star dominated on the previous models, the radiator grille was now organically integrated into the bonnet; the star, the only chrome decoration on the car, was supplemented by horizontal slats made of anodised aluminium. Significant improvements compared to the previous models have been achieved in terms of passive safety. With a great deal of development effort, it was possible to implement the exemplary safety standard achieved in the sedans and coupés in the SL as well. The lack of an umbrella organization required a body structure that differed fundamentally from the closed variants and thus required intensive development work. The desired dimensional stability of the passenger cell was achieved through the interaction of various structural elements. These included, above all, a particularly rigid floor system with high-strength sheet metal and large cross-sections as well as rigid transitions from the A pillars and the rear side parts to the side longitudinal members. The doors also played a crucial role. Thanks to the ingenious beading design on the inside of the door, they functioned as a pressure-resistant connection between the front wall and center pillar, thus helping to preserve the survival space in the event of a collision.
